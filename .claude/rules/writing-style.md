# Writing Style — All External-Facing Text

This rule applies AUTOMATICALLY to every piece of text the team writes that someone other than the builder will read: emails, LinkedIn posts, pitch decks, stakeholder updates, partner communications, document summaries, investor materials, and any public-facing copy.

This is NOT optional. Do not wait for `/humanizer`. These rules are always on.

Think of it this way: rules are DNA — they're part of every cell. Skills are tools — you pick them up when needed. This rule is DNA.

Internal explanations to the builder (teaching mode) follow different rules — see teaching-mode.md.

## Voice DNA

> **Customize this section.** Replace the descriptors below with ones that match the founder/team's natural speaking style. The defaults are a strong starting point for most founder-led projects. Update the banned phrases list with any cliches specific to your industry.

The builder writes like they talk: direct, confident, specific. A founder selling a real product to real people. Not a marketing department. Not a chatbot.

- **Direct.** Say what you mean. No warm-up paragraphs.
- **Confident but not arrogant.** State facts. Don't inflate them.
- **Specific.** Name the product, the number, the person, the date. Vague = weak.
- **Conversational.** Write like you'd talk to a smart colleague over coffee.
- **Varied rhythm.** Short sentences. Then longer ones. Fragments too. Mix it up.
- **First person when appropriate.** "I" and "we" are not unprofessional.
- **Acknowledge complexity.** Real humans have mixed feelings. Show that.
- **Have opinions.** Don't just report facts — react to them.

## 25 AI Writing Patterns — Always Detect, Always Fix

These patterns are the most common tells that text was AI-generated. Based on Wikipedia's "Signs of AI Writing" guide. Fix them automatically in every draft.

### Content Patterns

1. **Significance inflation.** Cut "testament", "pivotal moment", "vital role", "indelible mark", "setting the stage", "deeply rooted." State the fact instead.
2. **Notability inflation.** Don't list media outlets or follower counts without context. Say what was said, not just where.
3. **Superficial -ing phrases.** Delete "highlighting", "underscoring", "symbolizing", "showcasing", "fostering", "ensuring" when tacked on for fake depth.
4. **Promotional language.** Cut "groundbreaking", "vibrant", "stunning", "breathtaking", "nestled", "boasts", "renowned." Describe what the thing does, not how impressive it is.
5. **Vague attributions.** "Experts say", "industry observers note" — name the person or drop the claim.
6. **Formulaic "challenges and future" sections.** "Despite challenges... continues to thrive." "The future looks bright." State what's actually happening.

### Language and Grammar Patterns

7. **Overused AI vocabulary.** Replace or cut: Additionally, crucial, delve, enduring, enhance, fostering, garner, highlight (verb), interplay, intricate, landscape (abstract), pivotal, showcase, tapestry (abstract), testament, underscore, valuable, vibrant.
8. **Copula avoidance.** "Serves as", "stands as", "functions as" — just write "is."
9. **Negative parallelisms.** "It's not just X, it's Y." "Not only... but also..." Just state the point.
10. **Rule of three.** AI forces ideas into groups of three. Don't. Two is fine. Four is fine. Whatever fits.
11. **Synonym cycling.** Don't rotate "the platform", "the tool", "the system", "the solution." Pick one name and use it.
12. **False ranges.** "From X to Y" constructions where X and Y aren't on a meaningful scale.

### Style Patterns

13. **Em dash overuse.** Use commas, periods, or parentheses instead. One em dash per piece of writing maximum.
14. **Excessive bold.** Don't bold every key phrase. If everything is emphasized, nothing is.
15. **Inline-header bullet lists.** Bullets starting with "**Header:** description" is an AI tell. Use flowing prose or plain bullets.
16. **Title Case in headings.** Use sentence case. "Strategic partnerships" not "Strategic Partnerships."
17. **Emojis.** Never in external communications. Not in emails, not in LinkedIn, not anywhere.
18. **Curly quotes.** Use straight quotes.

### Communication Patterns

19. **Chatbot artifacts.** Delete: "I hope this helps!", "Let me know if you'd like me to expand", "Here is an overview of..."
20. **Knowledge-cutoff disclaimers.** Don't write "as of my last update" or "while details are limited." Say what you know or don't.
21. **Sycophantic tone.** Cut "Great question!", "You're absolutely right!", "That's an excellent point!"
22. **Filler phrases.** "In order to" -> "To." "Due to the fact that" -> "Because." "It is important to note that" -> cut it.
23. **Excessive hedging.** "It could potentially possibly be argued that it might..." — state it or don't.
24. **Generic positive conclusions.** "The future looks bright." "Exciting times ahead." End with something specific or don't end with an outlook.
25. **Hyphenated word pair overuse.** For common compounds (cross functional, high quality, data driven), drop the hyphen unless it genuinely aids clarity.

## Banned Phrases

> **Customize this list.** Add industry-specific cliches. These defaults cover the most common AI marketing language.

Never use these in any external writing:

- "Unlock", "Supercharge", "Revolutionize", "Game-changing", "Cutting-edge"
- "We're excited to announce", "We're thrilled to share"
- "In today's rapidly evolving landscape"
- "At the intersection of X and Y"
- "Leveraging AI" (describe what it does instead)
- "Seamless", "Robust", "Scalable" (without specific evidence)
- "World-class", "Best-in-class", "Industry-leading"
- "Empowering", "Transformative", "Disruptive"
- "Passionate about [industry]" (show it, don't say it)

## Two-Pass Rule

When drafting external text:

1. **Write the draft** applying all rules above.
2. **Self-audit:** "Does any part of this still sound like AI wrote it?" Check for uniform sentence length, soulless neutral reporting, overly tidy structure, missing personal voice.
3. **Fix what the audit caught** before presenting to the user.

## Context-Specific Guidelines

### Emails to partners / stakeholders
- Be direct. Peers are peers, not customers.
- Skip pleasantries beyond a one-line opener. Get to the point.
- End with a clear ask or next step, not a vague sign-off.

### LinkedIn posts
- Hook in the first line. LinkedIn truncates after ~150 characters.
- Write like you're telling a friend what happened. Not like you're writing a press release.
- One idea per post. No hashtag spam. Two or three max, at the end.

### Pitch materials (investors, partners, clients)
- Lead with the problem, not the solution.
- Use real numbers. "2,000 customers across 3 markets" beats "rapid growth."
- Name names. Specificity builds trust.
- Acknowledge what doesn't exist yet. Honesty about roadmap beats vaporware.

### Investor updates
- Lead with metrics, then narrative. Numbers first, story second.
- Bad news early, not buried. Investors respect candor.
- End with a specific ask if you have one. Don't waste the touchpoint.
